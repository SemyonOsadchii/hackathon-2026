Legal fact classification, from baseline to a small targeted correction

This page summarizes my hackathon work on Dutch legal document classification. The task was to predict multiple legal fact labels for each document, using the provided VectorMesh notebooks and cached embeddings as the starting point.

My final approach was not just a bigger model. The useful improvement came from paying attention to the metric, handling rare labels more carefully, and adding a small legal phrase router only where the error analysis showed a clear legal pattern.

The task I worked on

Each legal document can have more than one correct label, so I treated the problem as multi-label classification. Some labels are common, while others are rare. This made the task more interesting than a normal single-class prediction problem.

I used micro-F1 as the general quality check, but I focused more on macro-F1 during the later experiments. Micro-F1 can look good even when rare labels are ignored. Macro-F1 made those weak labels visible.

The final system stayed fairly simple. The neural part predicts label probabilities, thresholds are selected per label, and a small phrase router adjusts only a few closely related legal labels.

What became the final idea

I first thought the creative part would be a larger model or a reranker. The results pushed me in a different direction. The final idea was smaller but more connected to the task: use attention for the general model, then correct a narrow legal confusion pattern that the neural model struggled with.

The router did not try to fix every rare label. In fact, calibration rejected the phrase boost for labels 23 and 26, so I did not include it as a final improvement. That made the solution less flashy, but more honest.

Targeted router behaviour

WVG or voorkeursrecht boost label 28
Omgevingswet or 9.9 boost label 31
art. 7:3 without subtype small boost label 29

The selected correction was modest: a boost of 0.25 and a suppression of 0.15 for conflicting labels.

What the error analysis showed

Average F1 scores were useful, but the per-label table explained where the model was actually struggling.

Labels 23 and 26 stayed unresolved

These labels still had zero F1. I considered keyword features, but calibration did not support boosting them. The phrases were not clean enough and could easily add false positives.

Labels 28, 29, and 31 had a clearer pattern

These labels all relate to art. 7:3 BW, but differ in extra legal context. That made them a better target for a small rule-based correction.

The router improved the labels it targeted

Label 28 improved from 0.500 to 0.857 F1, label 31 from 0.417 to 0.842 F1, and label 29 improved slightly from 0.578 to 0.622 F1.

Reflection

The main lesson was that the best improvement did not come from simply adding a more complicated model. MoE sounded more advanced, but performed worse. The reranker was a reasonable idea, but did not add enough independent information. The useful progress came from looking at the metric, understanding the imbalance, and matching the method to the actual errors.

I would not claim the final solution is perfect. Labels 23 and 26 remain weak, and a separate final test set would be a stronger way to estimate generalization after all design decisions are frozen. Still, the final result improved both micro-F1 and macro-F1, and the final correction is connected to a concrete legal pattern rather than only to random tuning.
